The Question

Every top European league has a dominant team or small elite. But are the dynamics the same everywhere? Is the Premier League’s growing gap unique, or is stratification a continent-wide phenomenon?

Key Findings

TipSummary
  • All five leagues show a widening gap since 1992, but at different rates
  • The Bundesliga has the highest average gap (when normalized for season length), because Bayern’s dominance was already entrenched
  • Ligue 1 shows the most dramatic single inflection point (PSG takeover, 2011)
  • The Premier League and La Liga have the steepest rate of increase in the gap
  • Serie A is the most volatile due to Calciopoli and financial instability
  • No league has reversed the trend through regulation alone

League Summary Statistics

summary_rows = []
for key, df in league_data.items():
   config = LEAGUES[key]
   summary_rows.append({
       "League": config.name,
       "Country": config.country,
       "Seasons": len(df),
       "Teams": config.num_teams,
       "Avg Title Pts (38g)": round(df["Title Pts (38-game)"].mean(), 1)
           if "Title Pts (38-game)" in df.columns else None,
       "Avg Survival Pts (38g)": round(df["Survival Pts (38-game)"].mean(), 1)
           if "Survival Pts (38-game)" in df.columns else None,
       "Avg Gap (38g)": round(df["Gap (38-game)"].mean(), 1)
           if "Gap (38-game)" in df.columns else None,
       "Avg Ratio": round(df["Ratio"].mean(), 2),
       "Max Ratio": round(df["Ratio"].max(), 2),
       "Gap Trend (pts/yr)": None,  # Calculated below
   })
   # Calculate gap trend slope
   if "Gap (38-game)" in df.columns:
       x_num = np.arange(len(df))
       valid = df["Gap (38-game)"].notna()
       if valid.sum() >= 3:
           slope = np.polyfit(x_num[valid], df.loc[valid, "Gap (38-game)"].values, 1)[0]
           summary_rows[-1]["Gap Trend (pts/yr)"] = round(slope, 3)

summary_df = pd.DataFrame(summary_rows)
summary_df.style.format({
   "Avg Ratio": "{:.2f}x",
   "Max Ratio": "{:.2f}x",
   "Gap Trend (pts/yr)": "{:+.3f}",
}).hide(axis="index")

Decade-by-Decade Gap Evolution

decade_data = []
for key, df in league_data.items():
   config = LEAGUES[key]
   if "Gap (38-game)" not in df.columns or "Start Year" not in df.columns:
       continue
   df_copy = df.copy()
   df_copy["Decade"] = (df_copy["Start Year"] // 10) * 10
   df_copy["Decade Label"] = df_copy["Decade"].apply(lambda d: f"{d}s")
   for decade, group in df_copy.groupby("Decade Label"):
       decade_data.append({
           "League": config.name,
           "Decade": decade,
           "Avg Gap (38-game)": round(group["Gap (38-game)"].mean(), 1),
           "Color": config.color,
       })

decade_df = pd.DataFrame(decade_data)

fig_decade = go.Figure()
for key in league_data:
   config = LEAGUES[key]
   league_decades = decade_df[decade_df["League"] == config.name].sort_values("Decade")
   fig_decade.add_trace(go.Bar(
       x=league_decades["Decade"],
       y=league_decades["Avg Gap (38-game)"],
       name=config.name,

The Story

1990s: Relative Parity

In the early 1990s, all five leagues had relatively modest gaps between top and bottom. Title-winning totals were lower (70-85 points), and survival required more points (35-50). The financial playing field, while never truly level, was less extreme.

2000s: The Money Arrives

The Champions League’s expanding revenue model, combined with individual TV deals (La Liga) and oligarch ownership (Chelsea, Manchester City), began widening the gap. Title-winning points climbed while survival stayed flat.

2010s: The Acceleration

This decade saw the most dramatic changes:

  • PSG’s takeover (2011) created instant and massive inequality in France
  • Juventus’s nine titles (2012-2020) locked out all competition in Italy
  • City’s 100-point season (2018) and Liverpool’s 97-point response reset expectations in England
  • Messi and Ronaldo pushed La Liga’s top two to unprecedented heights

2020s: A Slight Correction?

The post-COVID era has seen marginally lower title-winning points in some leagues, but the structural gap remains. The top clubs’ financial advantages (Champions League revenue, global commercial deals, wage-spending capacity) continue to compound year over year.

Conclusions

  1. The divergence is universal. Every top-5 European league is more stratified in 2025 than it was in 1992.

  2. The causes are structural. TV revenue concentration, Champions League money flowing to the same clubs, and the compounding effect of success (better players lead to more revenue lead to better players) create a feedback loop that regulation has failed to break.

  3. The rate differs by league. The Premier League and La Liga show the steepest acceleration. The Bundesliga was already unequal. Ligue 1 had a single shock event (PSG). Serie A is the most chaotic.

  4. Survival requirements are universal. Regardless of league, ~33-37 points (38-game equivalent) is the survival threshold. The bottom of the table is constrained by the same dynamics everywhere.

  5. No regulation has worked. Neither the 50+1 rule (Germany), collective TV deals (Spain post-2016), Financial Fair Play (Europe-wide), nor the Profit and Sustainability rules (England) have reversed the trend.

Methodology

NoteTechnical details

Data source: All data scraped from Wikipedia’s season-by-season articles using requests and pandas.read_html().

Normalization: Since leagues have different numbers of teams (18 or 20) and therefore different match counts (34 or 38), raw points are not directly comparable. All cross-league comparisons use points-per-game (PPG) multiplied by 38 to produce a “38-game equivalent” that puts all leagues on equal footing.

Relegation definitions: To enable fair comparison, “relegation zone” is defined as the bottom 3 teams in all leagues, regardless of whether the 3rd plays a playoff (as in the Bundesliga and Ligue 1). “Survived relegation” is always the team immediately above the relegation zone.

Caching: Each league’s data is cached to a local CSV with 7-day expiry. The freeze: auto Quarto directive further ensures notebooks only re-execute when source code changes.

Code reuse: All five league pages and this comparison page share a common utility module (_utils.py) for scraping, enrichment, and charting, ensuring consistent methodology across the analysis.
